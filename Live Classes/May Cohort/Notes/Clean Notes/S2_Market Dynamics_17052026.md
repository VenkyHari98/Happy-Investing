## Price action follows fundamentals in the long run

- In the short term, price is driven by sentiment. In the long term, price follows fundamentals.
- **Kotak Mahindra example:** Stock consolidated for 4 years (Nov 2020 to Nov 2024), price barely moved. But during that same period, revenue nearly doubled and profit doubled. When a stock's fundamentals improve while the price stays flat or drops, that's an opportunity.
- Revenue tripled and profit quadrupled from 2017 to 2026 for Tech Mahindra, but the stock price only doubled. The gap between price and fundamentals is the opportunity.
- The market is forward-looking. It prices in problems 6 months ahead. By the time a problem is obvious, it's often already priced in.

## Why stocks fall (and whether it matters)

- Stock price changes come from three sources:
    - **Business reasons:** sectoral shifts, competition, innovation. These matter.
    - **Financial reasons:** company efficiency, debt, profitability changes. These matter.
    - **Price-only changes:** sentiment, trading dynamics, no fundamental change. These usually don't matter for long-term thesis.
- Temporary profit drops from raw material cost spikes (crude oil, rubber, metals) are not permanent. Crude oil prices cycle. Rubber prices cycle. If the underlying business is sound, margins recover.
- **Crude oil chain reaction:** crude oil price rises, India pays more in USD, INR depreciates, inflation rises, RBI holds/raises interest rates, consumer demand falls (delayed car/home purchases), company revenues dip. This entire chain is cyclical and reverses.

## Gold as a portfolio component

- Apply the same value-based approach to gold as to stocks: buy when undervalued, sell when overvalued. Not based on momentum or speculation.
- Recommended allocation: **10-15% of portfolio** in gold as a safety net during equity market downturns.
- Gold rally duration is uncertain. Probability favors a correction eventually.

## Inflation and interest rates

- India's target inflation range is **2-6%**. Deviations cause economic pressure.
- **RBI manages inflation through interest rates:**
    - Raising rates reduces liquidity, curbs inflation, but slows borrowing and spending.
    - Lowering rates stimulates borrowing, spending, business expansion, and supports market rallies.
- Lower interest rates reduce loan EMIs, boosting consumer spending and business expansion. This feeds into stock market performance.

## Strong players vs weak players

- **Strong players:** Promoters, FIIs, DIIs, HNIs, Government. They hold ~90% of market stake.
- **Weak players:** Retail public.
- Within strong players, two sub-groups:
    - **Investors (promoters, government):** hold for years/decades, want value appreciation, don't trade. They don't influence short-term price action.
    - **Traders (FIIs, DIIs, HNIs):** actively buy and sell. They move price action. They can put sell orders to push price down, buy orders to push it up.
- **HNIs** trade with their own money (e.g., Rakesh Jhunjhunwala, Radhakishan Damani). They benefit directly when stocks go up.
- **FIIs and DIIs** manage other people's money. Their income comes from **expense ratio** (typically ~1-1.5%) on AUM. When market falls 20%, AUM drops, their fee income drops, their profit drops ~30% because fixed costs stay constant. This is why they have a structural incentive to take the market higher.

## Market can go down but cannot stay down

- Quality companies (TCS, Infosys, Reliance, Bajaj Finance, Maruti, etc.) going to zero would mean the Indian economy itself has collapsed. At that point, your problem is food security, not stock prices.
- 90% of market stake is held by strong players. They will, by necessity, take the market higher because their businesses, income, and political stakes depend on it.
- Government has elections to win, jobs to protect, EMI cycles to sustain. If companies collapse, banking collapses, which collapses the economy. Nobody in power lets that happen.

## Market cap categories

- **Large cap:** Top 100 companies by market cap (roughly >1 lakh Cr). Established, lower volatility, moderate returns.
- **Mid cap:** Companies ranked 101-250 (roughly 34,000 Cr to 1 lakh Cr). Growth-oriented, moderate volatility, higher returns.
- **Small cap:** Below rank 250. Emerging, high volatility, highest return potential.
- Market cap category alone does not determine whether a company is worth investing in. Blue Dart (courier market leader) is small cap at 12,000 Cr. JK Tyres is 10,000 Cr. Exide is 29,000 Cr. Sector leaders can sit in any category. The opportunity is in the company's position within its sector, not its market cap label.

## Stock universe: F40, E40, S200

- **F40 (Flagship 40):** Large cap + some mid cap. The safest layer. Lowest volatility, lowest reward. **Start here.** Update very rare (4-5 years unchanged). All 10 strategies apply. Priority 1 for beginners.
- **E40 (Emerging 40):** Mid cap + small cap. Medium volatility, medium reward. First three strategies (long envelope, short envelope, 52-week high/low knock) do NOT apply to E40. Priority 2.
- **S200 (Smart Pick 200):** Small cap focused. Highest volatility, highest reward. Priority 3 (only after gaining experience). Envelope strategies do NOT apply.
- *SBI Bank exception:* SBI is a government bank but is included in F40 because it's the top beneficiary of financial sector growth. Government *companies* (Coal India, BPCL, etc.) are excluded from F40/E40. Government *banks* can qualify.

**Entry thresholds by market cap:**

- Large cap (F40): take opportunity after **20-30% fall** from highs
- Mid cap (E40): take opportunity after **30-50% fall**
- Small cap (S200): take opportunity after **45-60% fall**

## Six conditions for stock selection (F40 and E40)

These are qualitative filters, not quantitative screens. Companies are handpicked based on these six conditions.

1. **Debt-free or near-zero debt.** Debt-to-equity ratio should be negligible. Example: TCS has D/E of 0.11, borrowings of 11,000 Cr but cash equivalents of 12,000 Cr. Net debt is effectively zero.
2. **Market leader in their sector.** Dominant position with pricing power and competitive advantage.
3. **In business for 15-20+ years.** Longevity shows adaptability through political changes, economic cycles, and market shifts.
4. **Pricing power.** Ability to raise prices without losing customers. Companies that can pass on raw material cost increases to consumers.
5. **Future growth prospects for next 15-20 years.** Sectors where per-capita income growth and rising demand will sustain the business. Financial services, insurance, medical, automobile, energy are examples.
6. **Not a government company.** Government companies focus on equilibrium and regulation, not profit maximization. If SBI Bank didn't exist, HDFC would set minimum deposits at 50,000. If Indian Oil didn't exist, Reliance would push petrol to 120 Rs. Government companies exist to neutralize, not to maximize shareholder returns.

## Investing vs trading

- **Trading:** you have a target price to sell. Buy at X, sell at Y, exit, redeploy capital.
- **Investing:** no target to sell. Buy and hold for indefinite future.
- Long-term investing (buy and hold forever) in direct equities does not consistently beat 12-15% CAGR (roughly the nifty average). Evidence:
    - TCS 10-year CAGR: 6%
    - Asian Paints 10-year CAGR: 11%
    - Titan 10-year CAGR: 28% (exception, not the norm)
    - Reliance 10-year CAGR: 20%
    - HUL 10-year CAGR: 11%
    - Many quality companies deliver 9-16% long-term CAGR, barely beating mutual fund returns
- Long-term investing also carries the risk of picking a Reliance Power or DHFL that goes bankrupt. Not every stock is Titan.
- The community approach mixes trading (target-based entry/exit using strategies) with fundamental selection. This is how 25-30% CAGR becomes achievable.

## CAGR and the math of wealth

- **CAGR = Compounded Annual Growth Rate.** The average annual return when you look back over multiple years.
- Like average speed on a road trip: you might do 0 km/h at traffic signals and 180 km/h on the highway, but the average over the trip is 100 km/h.
- **Comparison across approaches (1 lakh invested for 6 years):**
    - FD at 6%: grows to ~1.42 lakh
    - Mutual fund at ~13%: grows to ~2.09 lakh
    - Strategies at ~30%: grows to ~5.33 lakh
- The volatility profile is different. FD is flat and guaranteed. Mutual funds range 5-20% annually. Strategy-based trading ranges -10% to +80% in any given year. The higher CAGR comes from accepting higher short-term volatility.

## SIP math and the power of small CAGR differences

- **Rs. 5,000/month SIP at 15% CAGR for 20 years = ~76 lakhs**
- Rs. 10,000/month = ~1.52 Cr
- Rs. 15,000/month = ~2.27 Cr
- Rs. 20,000/month = ~3.03 Cr
- For someone who doesn't want to trade directly, mutual fund SIP is still the best tool. 20,000/month at 15% for 20 years = 3+ Cr.
- But the CAGR lever makes a massive difference:

| CAGR | Rs. 20,000/month for 20 years |
|------|-------------------------------|
| 15% | ~3.0 Cr |
| 16% | ~3.5 Cr |
| 17% | ~4.0 Cr |
| 18% | ~4.68 Cr |
| 19% | ~5.43 Cr |
| 20% | ~6.3 Cr |

- **Each 1% increase in CAGR adds ~50 lakhs to the 20-year corpus.** This is why discipline, practice, and patience matter so much. The behavioral edge (putting GTTs on time, following the process, not skipping steps) directly translates into CAGR improvement.

## Shareholding pattern and price dynamics

- Price falls are not solely from retail selling. They come from overall supply-demand imbalances across all participant categories.
- During negative news (e.g., Adani group), promoters and institutional investors often do not reduce holdings significantly. Retail investors panic-sell. Big players capitalize on that fear.
- Check shareholding patterns on Screener to verify who is actually selling vs who is holding steady.

## Managing existing portfolio

- After learning all strategies (by session 8), review your old portfolio.
- If a stock fits a strategy and the target is hit or beyond, close the position and redeploy capital.
- If a stock is deep in loss, wait until it at least recovers to FD-equivalent return (e.g., bought at 100, target at least 112 for 2-year FD equivalent). Then close it.
- If the stock fits the strategy framework, you can average using the ABCD tranche system.

## Practical tips to keep in mind

- When you buy a stock, place the sell GTT the next day. Forgetting to place GTT locks your capital for months. This is not a 25% loss, it's months of opportunity cost. One instructor example: forgot GTT on Hindustan Unilever, missed the target, capital locked for 249 days, costing ~Rs. 20,000 in foregone returns.
- Envelope strategy (200 DMA +/- 14%): buy at lower envelope, sell at upper envelope. Works on F40 stocks. Does NOT work on E40 or S200.
- **Strategy-to-list matrix:** All 10 strategies apply to F40. Only some apply to E40 (no long envelope, short envelope, 52-week knock). Even fewer apply to S200. Don't apply strategies outside their validated list.
- Notes are more valuable than recordings for long-term reference. Use YouTube timestamp links in your notes (right-click video, copy URL at current time) so you can jump directly to the concept later.
- The gap between "I wanted to do it" and "I did it" is the secret of success. Wanting to place GTT vs actually placing it. Wanting to give feedback vs giving it. That gap is where money is made or lost.
