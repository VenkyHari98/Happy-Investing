## Six mistakes to avoid

- **No stop-loss (price-based):** exiting based on intraday or short-term drops without a pre-decided rule tied to business fundamentals.
- **Intraday trading (Fundo intraday):** frequent buying/selling on short-term signals. Intraday is a full-time business, not a part-time activity.
- **Investing borrowed money:** using leverage or credit to buy stocks. Magnifies losses. Called the deadliest mistake in this framework.
- **Concentration in one stock:** a large move in one name can damage the whole portfolio if sizing is wrong.
- **Panic selling:** selling because the market feels scary, without re-checking whether fundamentals actually changed.
- **Trading without a system:** following tips, influencers, or YouTube channels instead of a documented process.

Self-check before any trade: Did I set an exit rule? Is this borrowed money? Do I have a documented process for this trade?

## Position sizing

- **Per-stock caps by market-cap bucket:**
    - Small cap: max **1.5%** of portfolio
    - Mid cap: max **3%**
    - Large cap: max **5%**
- For a 10 lakh portfolio: max Rs. 15,000 in any single small cap, Rs. 30,000 in any mid cap, Rs. 50,000 in any large cap.
- These caps are non-negotiable. They protect against single-stock shocks and provide *emotional stability* when individual holdings fail.
- You can change overall bucket weights (aggressive = more small cap exposure, conservative = more large cap), but per-stock caps stay fixed. Size to the cap, not to conviction.
- Example: an aggressive 28-year-old with no dependents might go 40% small cap, 30% mid cap, 30% large cap. But even then, per-stock limit in small cap is still 1.5% (Rs. 15,000 on 10 lakh). Aggression changes the bucket mix, not the per-stock limit.
- Purpose: if a stock goes to zero, you should be able to watch it without losing sleep. At 1.5%, you can. At 10%, you can't.

## Stop-loss: fundamental, not price

- There is no price-based stop-loss in this framework. The stop-loss is placed *before* the buy decision, at the business selection and fundamental analysis level.
- Before buying, document what would make the investment thesis invalid. That becomes the exit trigger.
- Sell triggers: business model or competitive position changed materially, revenue/margins/cash flow deteriorating against expectations, new regulatory or governance issues that impair prospects.
- Price moves alone do not equal business failure. Confusing price with business quality is one of the most common errors.

## Discipline

- Discipline is the behavioral constraint that lets any reasonable strategy work. It controls how long you stay invested, which is what unlocks compounding.
- In practice: not checking the portfolio daily, not reacting to headlines, not overriding stops with conviction.
- If you decide not to check the portfolio for a week and you check on day 3, discipline is broken.
- Uninstall news apps (MoneyControl, ET Money, etc.). News only increases anxiety and triggers impulse buy/sell actions. The news that matters will reach you anyway. How to interpret it is what you learn in the cohort.
- **Decision quality is judged by whether the rules were followed, not by price movement.** If the strategy was followed and the stock drops 5% the next day, the decision was still right. If the strategy was ignored and the stock goes up 30%, the decision was still wrong.
- Compounding works only if discipline preserves holding time. In theory, compounding depends on time. In practice, discipline decides how much time you actually stay in the market.

## Compounding math (conditional on discipline)

- If strategy is followed with discipline: ~2x in 3 years, ~10x in 10 years, ~100x in 20 years.
- This requires 25-30% CAGR (roughly double the CAGR of mutual funds).
- This is not a salary. It's not 25% every year. Market does not work that way. Returns come unevenly across a 3-4 year market cycle.
- CAGR is an average over years when you look back. It's like average speed on a Chennai-to-Bangalore drive: 100 km/h overall, but you're stuck at 0 in city traffic and hitting 180 on the highway. Don't expect the average at every point.

## Four things in your control, two that are not

- **In your control:** what stock to buy, how much to allocate, the percentage allocation, and the buy/sell price.
- **Not in your control:** price volatility after you buy, and the holding time to reach target.
- Being aware that holding time is uncertain is itself wisdom. The right question is "what is my target price and I should wait" not "when will I get my profit."
- If you want certainty, that's available in FD (6% CAGR) or Nifty index (12% CAGR). The delta above that comes from navigating uncertainty with discipline.

## Trading journal

- The trading journal is the single source of truth. Not the demat app, not the news, not the portfolio screen.
- The demat account shows percentages that trigger emotional reactions. The journal shows structured decision criteria that tell you *when* to act.
- **What to record:** entry rationale (why this stock, which strategy), target price, live prices / distance from ATH, fall today, and explicit trigger actions.
- Journal-driven actions: place GTT when target reaches next 5% range. Place opportunity-trigger order if price falls by a defined percent and matches strategy.
- When confusion or anxiety hits, consult the journal. It overrides impulse. Discipline removes emotion. Emotion destroys discipline.

## Intelligence vs wisdom

- Intelligence is theoretical knowledge: financial ratios, balance sheets, definitions, terminology.
- Wisdom is knowing when and how to act on that knowledge in real conditions.
- A person with 15 years of market experience who knows every ratio but hasn't made money lacks wisdom, not intelligence.
- Economist example: economists know the most about money, but almost no billionaires are economists. Warren Buffett has an economics degree but didn't become a billionaire by applying academic economics.
- Knowing what free cash flow is = intelligence. Knowing it doesn't add significant value to *this* framework's decision-making = wisdom.
- Strategy can be taught in ~100 minutes (10 strategies, 10 minutes each). But changing mindset takes the entire cohort. The money is not made in the strategy; it's made in the mindset that lets you follow the strategy when fear and noise say otherwise.

## Ego and emotion

- Ego means insisting the market is wrong instead of updating your thesis. If you buy a stock and it falls, believing the market is wrong is ego.
- Market is supreme. You cannot go against the market. You can go against the crowd, but not against the market.
- Two kinds of people make money: those naturally humble enough to accept market outcomes, and those made humble by losing and learning.
- Even expert investors get 10 out of 100 decisions wrong. The wise ones ask "what did I do wrong" and course-correct. The egoistic ones defend the original position.
- Accept what the market gives this year, whether it's -9% or +40%. You can't decide what returns you get every year. The market decides.

## Operator psychology and engineered moves

- Panic and greed in retail investors are often engineered by large players who benefit when the crowd reacts predictably.
- When big headlines hit: stop, ask who benefits from this narrative, then check whether fundamentals or governance actually changed.
- **Canes/Motilal example:** Motilal gave a lower rating on one side and sold the stock on the other. The stock fell ~20%. The question is whether management/guidance changed materially or whether the move was engineered sentiment. Research "Canes Motilal" to see the pattern.
- The crowd sees only price action and the headline. The crowd doesn't see what Motilal is doing on the back side. Winning is not about predicting price; it's about understanding human behavior, crowd psychology, and emotional cycles.

## FII and DII dynamics

- FII selling Indian stocks doesn't mean the stock is bad. FIIs have different challenges: INR depreciation (~96 Rs per USD), better returns in US bonds (interest rates not coming down), capital rotation to Thailand, Korea, US markets where AI infrastructure wave is playing out first.
- DIIs are increasing holdings because Indian government restricts how much domestic institutions can invest abroad. If DIIs were allowed to invest freely overseas, everyone would sell INR for USD, rupee would crash from 96 to 110+, petrol prices would rise. Government manages this loop.
- FIIs and DIIs make decisions for their own stakeholders, not for yours. Don't get carried away by "FII selling" headlines. Understand *why* they're doing it and whether it affects your thesis.

## Sector rotation

- Big players have limited capital. They can't take all 5,000 stocks to all-time highs simultaneously.
- They rotate capital across sectors. This is why some stocks are at 52-week lows while others are at highs at the same time.
- Opportunities exist in every market regime because not every sector is in the same phase at the same time.
- In bullish markets, use strategies for stocks that are still at lower envelopes even when the broader market is running. Don't chase stocks that have already rallied beyond entry range. Work on the stocks where the setup exists.

## Sentiment vs fundamentals

- Sentiment plays out in the short run. Fundamentals play out in the long run.
- Use short-run sentiment to enter at favorable prices, then hold for long-run fundamental recovery.
- **TCS example:** 48-50% down from ATH. News says AI (Anthropic, etc.) will destroy Indian IT services. But historically, the first wave of any tech revolution goes to infrastructure, not applications. When the internet came, Dell/IBM/HP boomed first (hardware). Then Amazon/Google/Facebook came later. Same pattern now: data centers are booming first. Once infrastructure is built, the applications using that infrastructure will need servicing, and Indian IT (TCS, Wipro, HCL) will capture that opportunity. Financial institutions won't let AI take full accountability for critical operations. India is an inefficient country by AI standards, which means it's a massive market for AI products. TCS has been flat for 50% down, but FII and DII holdings show big players are not abandoning ship.

## Short-term drops vs business failure

- If the entry checklist was satisfied (business selection, financials, technical setup, entry/exit rules), a short-term dip does not mean the decision was wrong.
- **Irida example:** bought ~128, fell to ~120 same day (~3.5% intraday loss). With 65% upside potential and a 2-year holding horizon, there's no reason to panic. The strategy says it will come back. The same stock has rallied from similar levels before.
- **RR Cable example:** one-day drop ~20% after news that Aditya Birla is entering cable manufacturing. Competition entering is not the same as business failure. Someone who bought at the dip could have made ~100% in 433 days. But taking that trade requires courage backed by fundamental understanding, not just strategy knowledge.

## Return expectations

- Best ever long-term CAGRs globally:
    - Jim Simons: ~66% (arbitrage-based, market was inefficient in 1988-2018 era, not replicable now that markets are efficient)
    - Rak Jun: ~62%
    - Warren Buffett: ~20% (1965-2024 documented record)
- But Buffett's *early career* (1950-1960, small capital) saw ~50-58% CAGR. When capital was small, universe was huge, he found ridiculously cheap stocks and put $10K-15K into them. Once capital grew to billions, the problem changed: liquidity, market impact, capital protection. His later 20% is a different game from his early 58%.
- The community's approach mixes early Buffett (find discounted stocks with small capital) and Peter Lynch (find opportunity, enter, exit, repeat).
- Don't expect identical returns every year. Market cycles run 3-4 years. If the market hasn't given returns for 2 years, that means more potential upside remains in the cycle.

## IPO

- IPO is a system to transfer shares from known people (founders, early investors) to unknown people (public).
- Stay away from IPOs. Wait at least until the 200-day EMA has formed for actual price discovery. Before that, the price is perception, not discovery.

## Decision workflow: three trusted sources

- **TradingView:** price action, chart structure, signals. Price-based confirmation to enter, exit, or ignore news.
- **Screener.in:** company fundamentals and financials.
- **Selective business news (Economic Times, etc.):** macro events, company announcements. Use sparingly.
- These are the only two "sources of truth" needed for decisions. Everything else (MoneyControl, YouTube influencers, social media) is noise.
- **Fact vs opinion filter:** "Tomorrow it might rain" = opinion. "Yesterday it rained" = fact. Before any trade, ask: is this news a verifiable fact or a prediction? If opinion, don't change the plan. If fact, check TradingView and Screener, then follow existing trade rules.
- Second filter: who benefits from this news? If someone else benefits, take a decision, not a reaction.

## Envelope strategy (mentioned)

- **200 DMA +/- 14% envelope.** Buy at lower envelope, sell at higher envelope.
- Not all stocks offer lower envelope entry at all times. Don't fight a stock that hasn't given the setup. Work on the stocks where the lower envelope is already there.
- *Details will be covered in strategy sessions.*

## Rules are protection, not limitation

- Rules in this framework work like traffic signals. The red signal feels passive, but it prevents accidents.
- If you break the signal once and get away with it, you build false confidence and break it again. Eventually, probability catches up and the consequences are severe.
- Same with position limits, waiting in bearish markets, avoiding options/shorting. Breaking the rule once and profiting doesn't validate the rule-breaking; it increases the chance of catastrophic loss next time.
- In bearish markets: wait until the theme changes. Don't ask for strategies to make money in a falling market. That's ego. Recognizing the red signal and stopping is the expertise.
- Capital protection and wealth growth must be balanced. Like driving from Chennai to Bangalore with family: you care about reaching safely, not just reaching fast.

## Market regime and risk profile

- Same framework applies across profiles. What changes is bucket allocation, not per-stock caps.
- **Aggressive (e.g., young, single, no dependents):** 40% small cap, 30% mid cap, 30% large cap. Per-stock caps unchanged.
- **Conservative:** more weight to large caps. Fewer small cap positions.
- **Balanced:** middle ground.
- Strategies are same, rules are same, framework is same. What differs between people: behavior, discipline, practice, patience, time available, risk appetite, capital.
- Return range: 20-30% for disciplined followers of the framework. 30-40% for those who put in extra effort (additional research, time, volunteer work).

## Practical tips to keep in mind

- Uninstall MoneyControl, ET Money, and any app that gives free tips/news. These trigger anxiety and impulse trades.
- Reduce social media time. If you can't stop Instagram within your intended 5 minutes, that's evidence you need to change the environment. Replace one scrolling block with 30-60 minutes of study/practice daily.
- Stop consuming, start creating. Watching 3 YouTube influencers to validate a stock pick is not a system. The instructor lost 20 lakhs doing exactly this before finding a systematic approach.
- Focus, energy, and time are multiplicative. If any one of these is absent, the result drops. Social media steals all three.
- Ferrari and Lamborghini don't advertise on TV because their target audience doesn't watch TV. If your aspiration is financial freedom but your daily actions are TV/Instagram/YouTube consumption, the actions don't align with the aspiration.
- Cohort attendance, homework, and the 5:30 session time are design choices to filter for serious participants. Treat the cohort commitment as an investment in future portfolio capacity.
- Volunteering for assignments accelerates learning beyond passive attendance. Initial effort is higher but processes get easier over time.
- Direct mutual funds vs regular funds: the 1% commission difference, compounded over 20 years, can result in 2-3x difference in corpus.
- 70% of doubts in the next 3 months will come from this session's topics (mindset, discipline, behavior), not from strategy or fundamentals.
