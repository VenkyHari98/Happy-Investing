## 0. Mindset & Disclaimers (Before You Touch Any Number)

**The core principle of this entire session:**  
Everything we do in fundamental analysis is about *reducing the probability of failure and increasing the probability of success*. Nothing here is a 100% guarantee. If you want a guaranteed return, that is the FD (Fixed Deposit) — and nothing else.

**On Screener.in as a data source:**  
For a retail investor, Screener.in is the *best available source of truth* today. However, even this data is not 100% reliable — because the numbers originate from company-appointed CAs, who can (and sometimes do) manipulate figures. Frauds have happened at Indus Bank, Canara Bank, and many other companies. Being aware of this possibility is itself a form of protection.

**On portfolio allocation as a safety net:**  
Even if manipulation happens, your portfolio sizing protects you. The recommended allocation caps are:

- Large Cap → max 5% per stock
- Mid Cap → max 3% per stock
- Small Cap → max 1.5% per stock

The probability of fraud is lowest in large caps, medium in mid caps, and highest in small caps — the allocation sizing mirrors this risk gradient. Even if one or two stocks go to zero, the overall portfolio can still achieve ~30% CAGR over the long term.

**What we will NOT cover (and why):**  
Industry PE, Cash Flow statement, and most Ratios tabs on Screener — these are not practically used in this investing approach. The focus is exclusively on what gets *used in decision-making*.

## 1. Market Cap — The Most Misunderstood Number

### 1.1 What Market Cap Actually Is

**Formula:**

> Market Cap = Number of Shares × Current Price

Market Cap is simply the total price tag to buy the entire company today. Think of it like a whole pizza — Market Cap is the cost of the whole pizza. The Current Price per share is just the cost of one slice.

**Key relationship:**  
Market Cap and Current Price are the *same thing* at different scales. Market Cap is the company-level number. Current Price is the per-share number. Similarly: Book Value (total) vs. Book Value per Share, Equity Capital vs. Face Value — all follow this "whole pizza vs. one slice" logic.

**Reliance example (at time of session):**  
- Number of Shares: 1,353 crores  
- Current Price: ₹1,407  
- Market Cap: ~₹19 lakh crore (1,353 × 1,407 ≈ 19,03,671 Cr — minor difference due to timing)

**Important distinction:** Number of Shareholders (44.35 lakh people) ≠ Number of Shares (1,353 crore shares). Do not confuse these.

### 1.2 Shareholding Pattern — Who Owns the Pizza?

The shareholding pattern shows how the Market Cap pie is distributed:

- **Promoters** (Ambani family + sister concerns): ~50% — these are SEBI-designated insiders
- **FII (Foreign Institutional Investors)**: ~19% — money flowing from other countries
- **DII (Domestic Institutional Investors)**: ~19% — mutual funds, insurance companies within India
- **Government**: ~0.1%
- **Public**: ~10%

**Rule of thumb:** Public holding should ideally be less than ~30%. If it's higher (say 38-39%), there may be HNIs (High Net Worth Individuals) — anyone holding more than 1% of market cap is classified as an HNI. These are "strong players" or "operators" in the market.

### 1.3 What Does NOT Affect Market Cap (Critical Misconception)

**This is where most beginners get fooled:**

Any corporate action that does NOT change the Market Cap is NOT an opportunity to buy the stock. The three biggest traps:

**a) Stock Split:**  
If a ₹10,000 stock splits 10:1, you get 10 shares at ₹1,000 each instead of 1 share at ₹10,000. The Market Cap stays exactly the same — you moved a digit from one side of the formula to the other. No wealth was created.

**b) Bonus Shares:**  
If a company issues bonus shares (e.g., 1:1 bonus), the number of shares doubles but the price halves. Market Cap stays the same. The difference from a split is *where* the shares come from — bonus shares are carved out of Reserves (on the balance sheet) and added to Equity Capital. But the net effect on your wealth is identical: zero.

**c) Dividend:**  
If a ₹1,000 stock declares a ₹10 dividend, the stock will open at ₹990 on the ex-dividend date — the exchange automatically adjusts for this. This prevents "arbitrage" (buying before dividend, collecting it, selling at the same price). So dividend is also not a buying opportunity.

**Bottom line:** Stop chasing bonus, split, and dividend announcements. Many YouTube channels hype these events — ignore them.

### 1.4 Practical Impact: GTT Orders Get Cancelled

When any corporate action (split, bonus, dividend) occurs, **Zerodha automatically cancels your GTT (Good Till Triggered) orders.** You will receive a notification. You must then go to TradingView, check your target price, and re-place the GTT at the correct adjusted level. TradingView's chart lines don't auto-adjust, but your GTT does get cancelled.

### 1.5 TradingView: Always Keep "Adjusted" ON

In TradingView, there's an "Adjusted" toggle (hash-colored button, bottom-right area). **Always keep this ON.** When Adjusted is ON, historical prices are corrected for all corporate actions (splits, bonuses, dividends). If you turn it OFF, your support/resistance lines will show incorrect levels. This is critical because you and someone else could be looking at the same chart but disagreeing on price levels just because of this one setting.

### 1.6 Market Cap as an Opportunity Lens

This is the *practical* use of Market Cap — comparing growth potential:

**Asian Paints vs. Indigo Paints example:**

| Metric | Asian Paints | Indigo Paints |
|---|---|---|
| Market Cap | ₹2,38,000 Cr | ₹5,354 Cr |
| Annual Profit | ~₹3,700 Cr | ~₹144 Cr |
| Profit growth (doubling time) | ~9 years | ~4 years |
| 2000–2025 price growth | ~229x | N/A (newer) |
| 2014–2025 price growth | ~7x | ~48x (profit) |

**The child growth analogy:** A 2-year-old child grows dramatically between ages 2 and 6. A 35-year-old will barely change height by 60. Asian Paints is the mature adult; Indigo Paints is the growing child. The *rate* of growth is fundamentally different based on the starting base.

**Britannia vs. Mrs. Bector example:** Britannia (₹1.46 lakh Cr market cap) doubled profit from ₹1,000 Cr to ₹2,000 Cr in ~6 years. Mrs. Bector (₹8,000 Cr market cap) doubled from ₹70 Cr to ₹140 Cr in just ~4 years. Smaller companies grow faster — that's where multi-baggers come from.

**5paisa vs. Angel One example:** Both are in the same demat account business (below Zerodha and Groww). Angel One's market cap is ₹20,000 Cr while 5paisa is at ₹1,145 Cr — a 20x difference. Yet they operate in essentially the same market. The growth speed of the smaller player's profits will be dramatically faster.

**Key takeaway:** Don't look at *price*. Look at *Market Cap*. The opportunity for a ₹5,000 Cr company to become ₹25,000 Cr is far higher than for a ₹2.38 lakh Cr company to become ₹12 lakh Cr.

## 2. Three Types of "Price" — Current Price, Book Value, and Face Value

### 2.1 Face Value — Know It, Then Forget It

Face Value = Equity Capital ÷ Number of Shares

It's the price at which shares were originally issued when the company started. For an investor, Face Value has almost zero practical significance today. It's like knowing you bought a house for ₹10 lakh twenty years ago when it's now worth ₹2 crore — the original cost tells you nothing about its current potential.

**One trap around Face Value:** When media says "Company XYZ declared a 50% dividend," that 50% is calculated on *Face Value*, not Current Price. So a ₹10 face value stock giving "50% dividend" means ₹5 per share — which against a ₹3,000 current price is virtually meaningless. Don't fall for inflated-sounding dividend percentages.

### 2.2 Book Value — The Company's "Net Worth" on Paper

**Formula:**

> Book Value = Equity Capital + Reserves  
> Book Value per Share = Book Value ÷ Number of Shares

**What it really means:** If you liquidated all the company's assets and paid off all its liabilities, Book Value is what shareholders would theoretically get back.

**Titan example:**
- Equity Capital: ₹89 Cr
- Reserves: ₹11,535 Cr
- Total Book Value: ₹11,624 Cr
- Number of Shares: 88.8 Cr
- Book Value per Share: ₹11,624 ÷ 88.8 ≈ ₹131

Yet Titan's Current Price is ~₹3,500. That gap between ₹131 and ₹3,500 represents brand value, future earning power, and market perception — things Book Value cannot capture.

### 2.3 Why Book Value Is *Understated* (The Hidden Wealth)

**The Titan land example:** Titan's books show total land value at ₹163 Cr. The instructor went to Google Maps, measured the Hosur factory area (~8 lakh sq ft), and at even a conservative ₹4,000/sq ft, the land alone is worth ~₹345 Cr — more than double what the books show. And this is just *one* property; Titan has facilities across India. Book Value is calculated at *historical cost* (what was paid originally), not *current market value*.

**The coffee shop analogy:** You started with ₹10 lakh, built a brand over 5 years, now earn ₹10 lakh/year profit. Would you sell for ₹50 lakh (your total investment)? No — because you've created brand value, customer loyalty, efficient operations, and a profit engine. You'd ask for ₹2 crore. That premium above book cost is what the market pays for.

### 2.4 Book Value as a Practical Opportunity Signal

**When Book Value > Current Price → Something interesting might be happening.**

**Bajaj Hindustan Sugar example (actual trade taken by the instructor):**
- Book Value: ₹33/share
- Current Price at purchase: ₹22/share
- Current Price is *below* Book Value — rare situation

The company is a sugar manufacturer with 14 factories on fertile agricultural land (land that cannot be replicated — you need sugarcane-growing regions). The P&L looks terrible (losses everywhere), but the assets are massive and practically irreplaceable.

**The "Replacement Theory" (Harshad Mehta reference):** From the show "Scam 1992" — if someone wanted to build this company from scratch today, what would it cost? That replacement cost should be the true Market Cap. When a company trades far below its replacement value, it's potentially undervalued.

**Ethanol angle:** The instructor connected this to Nitin Gadkari's push to reduce India's petroleum dependence. Sugar companies would benefit enormously when ethanol blending mandates increase, because sugar factories already have the infrastructure. This is a 5-10 year play.

**However:** Book Value below Current Price is "good to have," NOT "must have." Many excellent companies (like Titan) will *never* trade at or below Book Value. Don't filter out great companies just because they're expensive relative to book.

### 2.5 Book Value Varies Dramatically by Business Model

**Capital-intensive businesses** (manufacturing, infrastructure) → High Book Value, large fixed assets. Example: Reliance has ₹10 lakh Cr in fixed assets + ₹2 lakh Cr in Capital Work-in-Progress.

**Asset-light businesses** (IT, trading, fund management) → Low Book Value. Example: TCS has only ₹23,000 Cr in fixed assets, Book Value of just ₹222/share. A fund manager with a laptop managing ₹1,000 Cr needs almost zero fixed assets but generates enormous profit.

This means you **cannot compare Book Value across industries**. A manufacturing company's Book Value and an IT company's Book Value are fundamentally different metrics.

## 3. Balance Sheet — The Building Blocks

### 3.1 The Liabilities Side

**Equity Capital:** The initial capital invested to start the company. Titan started with ~₹89 Cr. This is a liability *to the company* because it belongs to shareholders — the company must eventually return it.

**Reserves:** Accumulated profits over the years, after expenses. Year 1 profit goes to Reserves, Year 2 profit adds to Reserves, and so on. Reserves grow as long as the company is profitable.

**Shareholder's Fund = Equity Capital + Reserves** — this is what belongs to shareholders collectively.

**Why are Equity and Reserves classified as "Liabilities"?** Because a company is a virtual entity — it *owes* everything to its stakeholders. Equity Capital belongs to shareholders. Reserves are accumulated profits that also belong to shareholders. From the company's perspective, these are obligations to return.

**Borrowings (Debt):**
- *Long-term borrowing* — loans repayable after 1+ years (bonds, term loans)
- *Short-term borrowing* — loans repayable within a year (working capital loans, credit lines)
- *Lease liability* — a newer component (post Ind-AS accounting rules). Previously, rent was shown as an expense in P&L. Now, long-term leases are capitalized as a liability. **Do not panic about lease liability** — it's an accounting reclassification, not dangerous debt. When the company vacates the property, this liability disappears.

**Other liabilities:**
- *Trade Payables* — money owed to suppliers for goods/services received
- *Advance from Customers* — prepayments received for orders not yet delivered
- *Other Liabilities* — miscellaneous obligations

### 3.2 The Assets Side

**Fixed Assets:** Land, buildings, machinery, equipment, computers, furniture, vehicles — tangible, long-term assets.

**Intangible Assets:** Brand value, patents, goodwill from acquisitions. Example: HUL's intangible assets jumped from ~₹500 Cr to ~₹45,000 Cr in one year when they acquired the Horlicks brand. This is a perception-based value — it can rise or fall based on market sentiment (like Maggi's value dropping during the controversy). **For our analysis, we generally do not assign heavy significance to intangible assets.**

**Capital Work-in-Progress (CWIP):** Assets being built but not yet generating revenue. Like a highway "under construction" sign. Tata Electronics building a factory — until it starts production, the cost sits in CWIP. Once it generates revenue, it moves to Fixed Assets.

**Investments:** Companies invest too — FDs, mutual funds, equity, dollar hedging instruments. This appears as an asset.

**Current Assets:**
- *Inventories* — stock of goods/raw materials on hand
- *Trade Receivables* — money customers owe the company
- *Cash & Cash Equivalents* — the "cash register" of the business; how much liquid cash is available right now
- *Loans & Advances* — money lent out or prepaid
- *Other Assets* — miscellaneous

## 4. Debt-to-Equity Ratio — Is the Company Dangerously in Debt?

### 4.1 The Formula

> Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Borrowings ÷ Shareholder's Fund (Equity + Reserves)

**Titan example:**
- Total Borrowings: ₹20,777 Cr
- Shareholder's Fund: ₹11,624 Cr (89 + 11,535)
- D/E Ratio: 20,777 ÷ 11,624 = **1.79** (high!)

### 4.2 Net Debt — The More Meaningful Metric

**The personal finance analogy:** You have ₹1 Cr invested in mutual funds earning 12%. You need ₹20 lakh for 6 months. Instead of breaking your ₹1 Cr investment, you take a 10% loan for 6 months. You're technically "in debt" — but you're actually *better off* because your investment earns more than your loan costs.

**Net Debt = Borrowings − (Cash + Cash Equivalents + Liquid Investments)**

**HUL example:**
- Borrowings: ₹1,648 Cr (mostly lease liability)
- Cash & Equivalents: ₹7,000 Cr
- Net Debt: 1,648 − 7,000 = **Negative** → HUL is *virtually debt-free*

**Key distinction:**
- **Debt-free** = zero borrowings (rare)
- **Virtually debt-free** = borrowings exist, but cash/investments exceed them (common in good companies)

If Net Debt is negative, that's excellent for the company.

### 4.3 Custom Ratio on Screener

You can create a custom "Net Debt-to-Equity" ratio on Screener.in: go to Edit Ratios → Create Ratio → enter the formula. The instructor shared this formula during the session — replicate it from the recording. When this custom ratio is negative, the company is effectively debt-free.

## 5. PE Ratio — Return on Investment Timeline

### 5.1 What PE Actually Tells You

**Formula:**

> PE = Market Cap ÷ Net Profit  
> (or equivalently: Current Price ÷ Earnings Per Share)

Both formulas give the same result — the "Number of Shares" cancels out.

**What it means in plain language:** "At the current profit rate, how many years would it take for the company's earnings to equal my total investment?"

**Titan example:**
- Market Cap: ~₹3,07,800 Cr
- Net Profit: ~₹3,717 Cr
- PE: ~82

→ At current profit levels, it would take **82 years** to "earn back" the market cap. That sounds terrible — but here's why it's misleading...

### 5.2 Why PE in Isolation Is Misleading — The Britannia Proof

**Britannia in 2014:**
- Market Cap: ~₹9,733 Cr
- Net Profit: ₹395 Cr
- PE: ~25

Theory says: 25 years to recover. But *practically*, here's what happened:

**Year-by-year net profit:** 395 → 689 → 825 → 885 → ... → cumulative of ~₹12,500 Cr reached in just **5-6 years** (not 25).

Because profits *grow*, PE is just a snapshot at one moment. A growing company will recover your investment much faster than the PE number suggests.

### 5.3 Comparing PE Across Companies (Profit Doubling Speed)

**Asian Paints (PE ~61) vs. Indigo Paints (PE ~37):**

Asian Paints took **9 years** to double its profit from ₹1,800 Cr to ₹3,700 Cr.  
Indigo Paints took only **4 years** to double from ₹71 Cr to ₹144 Cr.

A lower PE doesn't automatically mean "better." What matters is the *speed* at which earnings grow. Faster-growing companies *justify* higher PEs because your return comes back sooner.

### 5.4 When PE Spikes — Is It Danger or Opportunity?

**Prince Pipes example (PE suddenly 158):**
- Profits dropped: ₹82 Cr → ₹43 Cr → ₹21 Cr
- Sales were actually growing fine
- The culprit: raw material (PVC resin) prices spiked → operating margin compressed → profit dropped → EPS fell → PE shot up

**The key question:** Is this *temporary* or *permanent*? PVC price spikes are cyclical and temporary. Once prices normalize, margins recover → profits recover → PE drops back to normal. This makes it an **opportunity**, not a threat.

**How to verify:** Go to the company's quarterly earnings transcript (Documents tab on Screener → Transcripts). Search for management guidance on when costs will normalize. Monitor quarter by quarter.

**Adani Wilmar (Fortune oil) — same pattern:** PE of 29 but profits collapsed due to palm oil price spikes (government policy + global supply issues). Temporary raw material issue → operating margin compressed. Once normalized, profits bounce back.

### 5.5 When PE Spikes Due to Fake Profit — The Paytm Warning

**Paytm declared ₹930 Cr profit in one quarter (September 2024).** Looks amazing on the surface. But:

- Sales didn't increase significantly
- Operating expenses didn't decrease significantly  
- Operating profit was actually down

**Where did ₹930 Cr come from?** Exceptional Income — Paytm sold its ticketing business for ~₹1,345 Cr. This is a *one-time* event, not recurring business income.

**Two types of "Other Income":**
1. **Normal Income** — FD interest, investment returns (recurring, predictable)
2. **Exceptional Income** — one-time asset sale, acquisition gain/loss (non-recurring)

If exceptional income inflates one quarter's profit, the PE will look artificially low. Next quarter, it'll be gone. **Don't buy based on a single exceptional-income quarter.**

**Mirror case:** If there's a one-time *loss* (exceptional expense), one quarter's profit will look terrible, PE will spike — but it's equally temporary. Don't panic-sell either.

### 5.6 PE Conclusion

PE being low is desirable but is **NOT a must-have condition**. Companies with high PE can still be excellent buys if their earnings growth rate justifies the premium. Always look *behind* the PE number — understand *why* it is what it is.

## 6. Profit & Loss Statement — Key Items to Watch

### 6.1 Revenue / Sales Growth

Check if top-line revenue is consistently growing. This is the first health check.

### 6.2 Operating Margin

> Operating Margin = Operating Profit ÷ Revenue

When raw material costs spike, operating margin compresses even if revenue is growing. This is the most common reason for temporary profit drops in manufacturing companies.

### 6.3 Net Profit vs. Operating Profit

Always check if net profit is being inflated by exceptional/other income rather than operating performance. The operating profit shows the *real* business health.

### 6.4 Quarterly vs. Annual Data

Quarterly data shows seasonality and recent trends. Annual data smooths out noise. Use both.

## 7. Chart & Observation-Based Opportunities

### 7.1 Fixed Asset Growth → Future Profit Signal

**Asian Paints example:**  
When fixed assets were ~₹3,000 Cr (2015-16), profit was ~₹1,800 Cr. When fixed assets tripled to ~₹9,000 Cr, profit also roughly tripled. Companies expanding machinery, land, and plants aggressively are investing in future production capacity.

**The logic:** If a coffee shop with ₹10 lakh investment earns ₹1 lakh profit, and the owner opens 3 more shops (₹40 lakh total investment), profit should grow to ~₹4 lakh. Good companies don't expand without confidence in demand.

### 7.2 Reliance Case Study — Validating Management Promises

Ambani committed (48th AGM) to doubling EBITDA by FY2027 (from ~₹1 lakh Cr in 2022). As of the session, EBITDA had already grown to ~₹1.65 lakh Cr. The remaining gap to ₹2 lakh Cr represents potential upside. When the stock price dips while the fundamental trajectory is intact, that's the opportunity window.

**The technical-fundamental handshake:** When chart patterns (like cup & handle) align with fundamental catalysts (EBITDA doubling commitment), the conviction level is highest.

## 8. Summary Cheat Sheet — What to Actually Check

### Must-Have Parameters (Deal-Breakers):
- Revenue growth trend (consistently growing?)
- Operating margin stability (not collapsing?)  
- Debt-to-Equity / Net Debt position (not dangerously leveraged?)
- Promoter holding trend (not declining?)
- Nature of any exceptional income/loss

### Good-to-Have Parameters (Adds Conviction, Not Mandatory):
- Current Price below Book Value → rare but attractive
- PE below industry median → indicates potential value
- Market Cap / Sales below median → efficiency signal
- Asset growth (fixed assets expanding = investment in future)
- 1-year / 3-year CAGR performance
- EV/EBITDA below median

### Watch-Out Red Flags:
- Interest costs eating into profit (growing faster than revenue)
- Borrowings increasing without corresponding asset/revenue growth
- Exceptional income inflating a single quarter's results
- Tax payments being manipulated (verify in transcripts)
- Book Value "opportunity" that isn't backed by business potential (don't grab blindly)

## 9. Key Mental Models from This Session

1. **Pizza Analogy:** Market Cap = whole pizza cost. Current Price = one slice cost. They're the same thing at different scales.

2. **Child Growth Analogy:** Small companies (children) grow fast. Large companies (adults) grow slowly. Capture the growth phase, not the maturity phase.

3. **Coffee Shop Analogy:** Book Value is what you *spent*. Market Value is what someone would *pay* — which includes brand, effort, systems, and profit engine.

4. **Replacement Theory:** What would it cost to build this company from scratch today? If that number far exceeds the current Market Cap, the stock may be undervalued.

5. **Personal Loan Analogy:** Debt isn't always bad. If your investment earns 12% and your loan costs 10%, the loan is *creating* value. Same logic applies to companies.

6. **Forward-Looking PE:** PE is a rearview mirror. What matters is the *speed* at which the company is driving — look at profit growth rate, not just the current PE number.
